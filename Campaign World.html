Rive

A mercantile country with a nominal diarchy, Rive was once as mighty as any other and is as beautiful as a polished jewel. Its wealth is vast, and there is an enormous societal gap between the rich and poor. Slavery is common here, uprisings ruthlessly suppressed, and loyalty bought.

The current King of External Affairs and Queen of Internal Affairs are married and hold as little real political power as their many predecessors have for as long as most can remember. They want to restore the crown's weight and the glory of their country in foreign countries' eyes.

Size

Biomes
  • Largely temperate rainforests, with some wetlands near the coasts to the east and tropical rainforest to the south.
Weather patterns
  • Usually warm and sunny with regular rainfall. Temperatures average between 54℉ and 64℉ across most of the country except for in the tropical rainforest, where it averages 72℉.

Currency

  • The units of currency are called Royals (solid gold coins), Nobles (gold covered coins), and Strata (silver coins), and their values are determined by their weights for Nobles and Strata, and by royal decree for Royals. Different denominations are made with different ratios of lighter metals layered in gold, or simply lighter metals.

    • 1 Royal = 1,200 Nobles
    • 1 Noble = 12 Strata
    • Denominations of 1, 2, and 6 for all. A loaf of bread is ~2 Stratum, a lamb is ~8 Nobles, shoes about the same, slaves ~200-300 Nobles, houses 400-1,000 Nobles.

Culture

In Rive, money can buy everything but happiness, and gold and gems flow like water.

Child Rearing
  • Children are raised to respect their parents absolutely, never speaking back to them, and taking care of them when necessary. Until they reach the age of 16, they are under their parents’ authority, and can be sent to work for their parents to collect the wages in exchange for being kept. Despite this, children are rarely abused, and there are strict laws punishing domestic violence. As well, adopted children are legally the same as blood children, and boys and girls inherit the same. Children are rarely sold into slavery, only in dire circumstances.
Names

  • Riven have only one name, with attached lineage or titles tacked on the end.

    • For example, the King Lugalbanda might be introduced as "Lugalbanda, Utu's Son, Ninsun's Husband, King of External Affairs."
Earning
  • Riven citizens usually work in the Guilds or as soldiers.
  • Pay varies greatly, but 6 Stratum a day is the minimum for a viable living. Skilled workers could make 1 Noble a day or more.
Learning
  • Schools are only attended by the wealthy and are taught by scribes. Students learn about geography, mathematics, language, history, and economics. Private tutors also exist and are highly respected individuals.
Style

Rive is well known for its megalithic structures, artistic writing, vibrant textiles, and heavy jewelry.

Language

  • Riven largely speak Riven, though traveling merchants and wealthy and educated people tend to be at least literate in one or more other languages. The language, like the nation, is focused on wealth, so the worth of a noun, monetary or otherwise, is built into the way they are said, just like verb tenses are in English.

    • Riven is an agglutinative subject-object-verb language, and its words tend to be one to three syllables.
    • It’s written either of two ways, logographic or phonetic scripts. The logograms depict a wide variety of images, similar to hieroglyphs. The phonetic script looks like a long and decorated line of ink, such as tibetan or arabic scripts. Both are written with quill, or carved into clay or stone.
Stereotypes
  • Riven are stereotyped as being greedy and holding grudges.

Culture

In Rive, money can buy everything but happiness, and gold and gems flow like water.

Child Rearing
  • For serfs, suburban citizens, and other non-nobility, families tend to be tight-knit, and children are raised mainly by their parents with some close relatives helping now and then.
  • For the nobility, families are often small and tense since they have to worry about succession, inheritance, securing ties, etc., and parents usually don’t have much time to spend with their children.
  • For the imperial family, there are often more children than fingers on both hands, and maybe a third as many women in the harem, all vying for power, prestige, the Emperor’s approval, and right to succession.
Names
  • Austere serfs have one name, a personal name their parents give them two weeks after birth.
  • Citizens usually have two names, one family and one personal. For example, one could be named “Onyáma Naka,” with “Naka” as a personal name and “Onyáma” as a family name.
  • Nobles may have a third name to keep track of lineage in a large family.
  • Imperial family members have three names: the imperial family name, their other parent’s personal name, and their own personal name.
  • Except for serfs, the personal name is the most private, and only those most dear address one by it.
Earning
  • The rural population is largely serfs, so their pay is food and protection. Suburban and urban Austere citizens earn money through a variety of crafts and trades, from silk-spinning to masonry to mercenary work. Nobility gain income from taxes, trade deals, and investments. The imperial family gains money through taxes.
Learning
  • Schools are only attended by the sons of noble families. Noble daughters are privately tutored. Imperial sons and daughters are also privately tutored, but they are often tested with real practice.
Style
  • The Empire is well known for its distinctive language and script, its ornate textiles and clothing styles, and its impressively complicated rituals and traditions.
Language

  • Austere people speak Austere, though a few state employees and traveling salesmen speak other languages. This is a language that emphasizes nuance and respect, with a word for every family member, and a number of formal and informal honorifics.

    • It’s an analytic language with a subject-verb-object basic word order. It’s also tonal, so pitch is important for distinguishing syllables.
    • It’s written with a mix of phonetic and logographic scripts with a brush and ink.
Stereotypes
  • The Austere are often stereotyped as snooty, uptight, and athletically inferior.

Croft Republic

A young democratic republic, only a hundred years old, with the highest position of “President.”

  • Each town is part of a “region,” which is part of a “territory,” which is part of the Croft Republic. Regional and territorial administrative officials are elected by locals or appointed by the president, and regional and territorial leaders elect the president.
  • The President is practically a King for their years in office, except when their decisions are overruled by more than half of the territorial leaders in peacetime. In wartime, they have supreme power.

120 years ago, this land was a mess of squabbling minor countries not unlike the feuding clan territory to the north, but eventually it was united through conquest.

  • An ambitious young lady led her army from the east to the west and either killed or convinced each ruler she crossed to capitulate and join her growing republic.
  • She did not treat her fallen enemies cruely, outlawed slavery altogether, and encouraged mixing cultures and people to unify the people.
  • When she had conquered enough, she wrote that she should be succeeded through election, and died 6 years thereafter. Every president since has had a single 6 year term, elected as currently through convention.

Eruditia

A harsh magocratic technocracy with an utterly alien culture to the rest of the world, Eruditia is ruled by a small collection of powerful mages, known as the nobility, who live in the floating Citadel.

There is one driving purpose: learning. To that end, all Erudites are given a generous stipend, and if they are magically talented, given nearly free reign to research their chosen pursuits. Further resources are provided with sufficient justification, and results are highly valued. With significant accomplishments, one might join the ranks of the nobility.

For normal people, it may be said that "upon the event of your death, you are dead." Not so for the nobility of Eruditia, whose contributions to the knowledge and understanding of reality are more valued than their humanity. Upon death, the nobility take over the bodies of unproductive members of society in order to continue their work indefinitely.

  • Although there are those who are incapable of serving physically or magically, everyone who is able in at least one of the two is expected to take up arms when called for.
Treaties and Agreements
  • The country’s diplomatic policy is very hands off. They basically don’t bother their neighbors and their neighbors don’t bother them.
Military Equipment
  • Military equipment is modern and regularly maintained.
Military Service Requirements
  • Military service is not mandatory outside of war.

Economy

Unemployment Rate
  • 47%.
  • Those in the Citadel are all employed somehow, either in government positions, as students, supporting infrastructure, et cetera. Those on the ground do sometimes choose to work, but most of their needs are seen to until they use up their resources.
Poverty Rate
  • 0%.
  • They all have access to plentiful resources, but those who run out quickly die of starvation or are cannibalized.
Literacy Rate
  • 100%.
  • Everyone is educated growing up in a nursery.
Technological Capability
  • Technological capability is above average due to inclination towards innovation.
Natural Resources
  • Natural resources are outsourced and include: ... almost everything
Currency
  • None.

Culture

Child Rearing
  • Every Erudite is raised in a collective nursery, not by their biological parents. Here they are educated on their history and purpose, as well as general subjects including mathematics, science, linguistics, art, and more. And of course, they are all tested for magical aptitude.
Names

  • Erudites are given a name by the nursery they grew up in, the year they were born, and a changeable name that eventually settles as they grow, usually as something they are known for.

    • For example, one might be named “Unomi 5333 Wiseman” for being raised in nursery Unomi in 5333 and being particularly wise. Successful members of the Academy are also given names for their success, usually corresponding to their specialty, such as “the Negotiator.”
Earning
  • Erudites are all allocated the same resources to start with, accessible by request, which they may use freely for the rest of their lives. Nobody uses money.
Learning

  • Everyone is taught the basics of magic if they can manage it.

    • Those who are not talented with magic may live out their natural lives on the ground. They may at any time be utilized by mages in the pursuit of progress.
    • Those who can use magic proficiently are brought to the Academy in the Citadel to learn and advance understanding, although there is significant self study to encourage unique and original perspectives.
Style
  • There is little official unity in fashion, arts, or craftsmanship. Originality, utility, and advancement are highly valued.
Language
  • Earthly Erudite mostly speak Mondri.
  • Most educated Erudites are literate in multiple foreign languages but increasingly, with rank, education, and age, they tend to use a strange form of telepathy to facilitate extremely rapid and exact communication. This unique telepathy is not taught, but rather learned through exposure, and can be difficult-to-excruciating to receive and understand if unskilled.
Stereotypes
  • Erudites are often stereotyped as being reckless and blunt.
